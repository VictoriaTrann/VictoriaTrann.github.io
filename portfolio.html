DR. DRONE MOBILE APP

Branding & UI/UX

Dr. Drone was the result of a Sydney University undergraduate project, as part of the DECO2200 Interaction Design Studio class. This was completed through a team collaboration with two of my classmates, Rachel Ryoo and Josh Townsend, with the guidance from our design tutor, Ashlyn Lee.

My design team (Untitled) and I took an emergency streamline service related challenge through various stages of user experience design, from discovery and ideation to storyboarding and personas to prototyping and user testing. This effectively guided our team to observe the potential innovation space in the efficiency of the paramedic industry, directing the outcome of our solution.

Project Overview

The Challenge

With the heightened expectations and demands of emergency services in being able to deliver minimal response times, the efficiency and ability of the emergency streamline services in extending help to patients is essential.

Combining the design brief of mapping out the interactions that will be required to assist people in interacting with autonomous vehicles in future cities within the next 10 years, my team and I intended to find an alternative means of assistance that removed some of the burden on the paramedical and emergency teams, but still kept their needed skills and responsibility intact.

The Solution

Our proposed solution was first a design concept to enhance the paramedics’ work efficiency, through the dispatchment of a first aid drone. It was then iterated and became a service design concept. “Dr. Drone”: a platform for quick emergency intervention, placing the power in patient’s and bystanders’ hands. The novices could now also track the journey of the emergency services live, whilst getting on the spot connection and support from the typical 000 emergency call centre.

What is Dr. Drone?

Dr. Drone is an assistive and informative emergency app that allows users to be able to search quick accurate medical solutions, and request immediate help from the palm of their hands. The app will automatically connect users to an autonomous ‘Dr. Drone’ that will arrive on scene to diagnose and provide basic personalised medical attention, before the arrival of the emergency teams. The innovation here is to position patients, bystanders and novices to feel in control and empowered under stressful situations. This will simultaneously reduce the enormous burden on paramedics, and increase patient survival rates.

The Process

Discovery & Understand

I conducted background research on the paramedic industry, helping to identify current issues. I additionally outsourced some videos on a patient’s perspective to learn from those outside of our immediate focus.

Our team prepared questionnaires, interviews and conducted observations on stakeholders (with working and training paramedics, witnesses and patients of an emergency situation involving paramedics, and working nurses).

Figure 1.1 - Interview

I’d like to particularly give a big appreciation mention to Daniel, a clinical director and active frontline paramedic at Paramedics.net.au from Queensland, who was actively involved in this process and provided valuable industry insights to me.

Research: Analysing Competitors

I looked into 3 already existing designs (Defibrillator Drone, Paramedics Rescue Pac, CPR Vest) to further identify insights and valuable information into current solutions for the paramedic industry.

“How can we help the paramedic streamline service to provide a faster and more accurate on-the-spot intervention, without interfering with the essential emotional support from a paramedic-patient relationship?”

Our team then used general sketches and wireframe sketches to gather overall ideas, and visually communicate the physical assets and interactions of our final proposed solution.

Design (prototype & user testing)

Figure 5.1 - Paper Prototype

The paper prototype was used to conduct user testing (through the think-aloud protocol, co-discovery and post semi-structured interviews).

Figure 5.2 - Affinity Diagram

Affinity and heuristic evaluation was used to synthesise user feedbacks and identified design issues. This drove our Dr. Drone mockup design direction.

Implement

Now that my team and I have defined our UX guidelines from user testings, this was reflected in the medium fidelity prototype. This allowed us to consider what content would be relevant and useful, and allowed us to work on the information architecture. We were also able to start implementing functionality and build experience.

Figure 6.1 - Medium Fidelity Prototype

Evaluate

We conducted more user testing and bodystorming activities for the medium fidelity, and refined our insights using more affinity diagrams and heuristic evaluation to influence our next stage of higher fidelity. This stage helped my team and I to identify improvements for user experience and user interface regarding the user flow. Our final prototype look was a result of all of this.

Key Learnings

  • Next Steps: User test on our secondary users, paramedics, to further flesh out the effectiveness of Dr. Drone and iterate where necessary.
  • My team and I lacked planning with the paramedics, particularly as this was a university project with time constraints due to tight deadlines. This taught us the importance of clear communication and organisation with users in a busy industry at the beginning of a product cycle, in order to fully utilise key resources and feedbacks for the service you are creating.
