Process Journal

Introduction

This process journal contains an outline of the overall design process that my group and I went through for our University of Sydney undergraduate project, as part of DECO2200: Interaction Design Studio. It contains our ideations, research, user testings, evaluations and final implementations for our chosen design scenario: the streamline service of the paramedic industry. Additionally, this journal will show personal behind the scene insights of some of the struggles, victories, and lessons that my team and/or I came across.

Definitely a real picture of my design team: Untitled

I also talk about the identified potential problem spaces that we came up with through research, which guided our final specific problem statement. This created more clarity with our team’s direction and goals, which was further explored, regarding ideation, through brainstorming and sketching. My process journal will also be able to provide an insightful walk-through of how we worked as a team, and how we got to our final destination with our 3 strongest solutions - which were then narrowed down to our one final solution. All of these iterations and explorations were essentially encapsulated through low to medium prototyping fidelities. The final concept was then taken to a high fidelity mockup, with the guidance from previous feedbacks through our design process and earlier wireframes/prototypes.

1. Our starting point: The Design Brief

The overarching focus of our design project was on designing and prototyping screen-based interfaces that enable interactions with autonomous vehicles in future cities, within the next 10 years. So our first task was to identify a problem scenario or a type of autonomous vehicle that we’d want to use. As a team, our first action was to look into current autonomous vehicles and the potentials of a ‘smart city’ that would use autonomous systems. This helped us to generally understand how others have implemented autonomy to assist people, and to help us gain some ideas of the problem area we wanted to work with. For me personally, this activity helped me to better understand what AI technologies can do, and what autonomous vehicles are. Frankly, I actually had no idea what autonomous technologies were before starting this project! So seeing current examples, like an autonomous friend robot, helped me to vision it physically and understand the features of autonomous devices better.

Figure 1.1 - Josh and I are pictured researching by Rachel

From looking into current technology examples, and discussing about some everyday problems that we each have faced or have seen happened, my group began to collectively categorise our insights into 2 potential problem areas: Women/personal safety, and children’s education. However, this stream of thought, through further elaboration, was short-lived and was then diverted to the paramedic/medical industry. This decision was mainly through having found an overall area that all team members quickly became passionate about. I personally like that we diverted into this area of thinking, as I felt that it made us more united as a group, increased our motivation and also helped to portray a clearer potential shared vision of where we’d like our design to head towards. This clarity and collectivism hence drove us to conduct further primary and secondary research into our context over the next few days. This solidified our team’s potential directed design area of attempting to improve the efficiency of a paramedic’s process in reaching the patient and getting them back in time to the hospital. Personally, I was actually very excited to dive into this industry as it would relate to the health industry - which is an area that I’m thinking of taking my design education towards!

Now that we had a potential pathway to head towards, we decided to have a quick brainstorm session to warm us up into working as a team. It was quite fun coming up with absurd ways to help the paramedic industry, and we did have a lot of fun together here. At this stage, we were very excited to dive into our studio project together!

The next stage was explored through Task 1 of our assessment: an individual research report. This would provide the bulk of our background research to enhance our design solutions for our context. The background research allowed me to understand the context on a deeper level, and to identify real problem spaces in the paramedic industry. We then broke off to individually research and navigate our own ways around our chosen context. I started my task by reading articles about the importance of streamline services. From this, I gained a general insight into the trend in our growing and ageing population and the heightened expectations and demands of emergency services from a changing social and environmental behaviour. I saw that this essentially was the driving force behind the potential design space for our project. So as a result, my objective for my research report became ‘To improve the efficiency and ability of the streamline of paramedic services in extending help to patients, and hence overall increase survival rates’.

I then picked 3 precedent designs to study (Defibrillator Drone, Paramedics Rescue Pac, CPR Vest) which helped me identify a common theme to them to help me develop a stronger foundation for my concepts, and to also identify any existing issues. These insights were:

  • The main theme relies heavily on trying to cut back time to increase survival rates. This shows a potential area of needed improvement and of importance in this industry.
  • I observed that there was a gap for holistic approaches in the paramedic industry.

  • Improve traffic and road conditions
  • Have more holistic approaches

The paramedics shared a lot of insights into why it may not be able to work, or that they already had this particular system in place that works well. 3. First development of our ideas!

After having completed the previous task individually, we came back together with a stronger mental map and idea of our problem area. I remember thinking at this point how interesting and beneficial it was for all of us to have completed the first task individually, and then to come back together to share our different journeys and findings from the same chosen context. I had originally thought that we would come back with roughly the same idea, but it was quite interesting to see that not only did we all have completely different insights, but our different approaches to the context had led us to all essentially have highlighted 2 more refined problem areas from our general problem context. This helped to guide us a lot in designing and ideating solutions that were more specific to our intended target users: front-line paramedics and/or patients and bystanders. These 2 areas were:

  • Improving technology for dangerous and hard to reach situations
  • Improving the transportation process

Our group then decided to synthesise all our different findings into 3 main requirements, which would act as the foundation of our ideation stage. These requirements were:

  • The need for the design to address the expanding diversity of arising situations, particularly hard to reach situations
  • The requirement for the design to avoid removing total human interaction between paramedics and patients
  • The ability for the design to add to a more efficient routine and intervention from paramedics, through easy to use and understand designs

I loved that we all synthesised our findings and/or key insights into 3 main dot points, because for me, it really helped me to think more clearly whilst not straying away from the important requirements that need to be kept in mind. This was then able to allow me to think more outside the box, and I found myself starting to throw more ideas and concepts out to my group, as well as further expanding upon their ideas, in an effective way.

At this stage, it was now all about brainstorming potential concepts from our two refined problem areas, whilst keeping in mind our outlined 3 main requirements for our user target. This was able to unite our group together regarding our problem space, through being able to prompt collective deepening of our understanding of potential concepts and issues, which enhanced the quality of our ideas. Using our general brainstorm, which effectively showed us what kind of direction our team seemed to be heading to, we then decided to come up with a more specific problem statement to better guide our ideation stage. From creating this specific statement, I found that it better guided me to understand our overall project. At this point, I felt ready and re-energised to ideate better potential design solutions within our now clearer design problem space.

Although this does take the burden off paramedics, and would act to be more efficient in getting to the patient and offering them quick help, it fails to incorporate the key insight my team came across in our user research: the comfort of having the human interaction from a paramedic-patient relationship. 6. User Testing our 3 concepts.

Overall, through user testing and synthesising insights gained from them using affinity diagrams and heuristic evaluations, we were able to gain a deeper and broader understanding of each of our 3 chosen design concepts. This process of having to test our services on other people also helped me to realise some of our team’s poorly designed decisions - as in the stage of conducting the user testings for all 3 concepts, I caught myself not actually knowing how to explain certain steps/navigations to the users when they needed it. This was particularly evident for the first 2 concepts, where the lack of structural guidance and intuitive interactions led to an overall confusing user experience.

Figure 6.1 - Using affinity diagrams to synthesise our results for all 3 concepts

The overall insights/findings for the Zone MediKit Drone can be summed up below:

  • We realised that the feasibility within the 10 year max time frame was compromised
  • We learnt about the importance and iterations needed to enhance the value of visual hierarchy, and its effect on usability
  • We realised we needed to visually show the assumed and much needed hierarchy of having the most important updates first, and then the least towards the end
  • We needed to ensure consistency of menu locations
  • We needed to have a way to make sure users visually understand what information is on the screen and what is not (and also what is available at what point to use)
  • We need to better decide when to use icons and when to use text to display information better, and to communicate the button’s affordance better

The overall insights/findings for the Autonomous Drone Ambulance can be summed up below:

  • We realised that although this concept was easier to use than concept one, due to better hierarchy of information and layout, its concept is the weakest out of all of our 3 concepts in regards to its ability to address the context of our problem statement/design space.
  • We also learnt that the holistic scanning potential is unlikely going to exist in the next 10 years.
  • The importance and reliance on information to be displayed and accessed in the correct way to enhance quick navigation in a time sensitive matter. This made us realise how important the icon designs are, and made us realise that there were certain sections that would be better communicated through text descriptors.

The overall insights/findings for the Dr. Drone App can be summed up below:

  • We needed to have the ability to confirm the activation of the dispatching ability of immediate help
  • Positive feedbacks were gained in regards to the ability for the quick help to intervene and decrease the risks of time-sensitive situations, the inclusion of having constant emotional support and guidance from the user’s phone as well as being able to converse with real life emergency people
  • The need for an immediate and accurate confirmation of patient’s location
  • We needed to improve the button placements, clarity of information and instructions, and error protection (in terms of useability).
  • We needed a way to more accurately hone the designed service’s usefulness

Overall, the user testing for our three concepts saw my team and I gain a better understanding of our design space and how the effectiveness of the ability of our designs to help our users. The experience from this was very insightful and interesting, and it confronted us with the errors of some of our way of thinking and approaching our problem space. Although it was confronting and some of it made us stressed as a team (emphasise on the word stressed), it was able to allow us to break away from our tunnel vision approach. The biggest confrontation was in the realisation that our concept 1 and 2 (both designed for a paramedic to use) was going to be very complicated, particularly with the absence of paramedics to test out the usability and hence also usefulness of our concepts. However, this challenge only fuelled us up more and soon, our focus and determination were all directed into making concept 3 stronger. We were excited that with this concept, we could actually test on the right users and hence gain more valuable and specifically insightful feedbacks from them.

7. Rapid Prototyping to medium fidelity!

This section will explain why my team went for concept 3 (Dr. Drone app) as our final decision, which will then lead into the process of us creating the medium fidelity of this concept for further user testing! For me, this stage was a huge relief. Our team was reaching the end of our ropes and really struggling to make a comeback after we discovered that our marks would be cut by not testing with primary users. Before this point we had tried for well over a month to try and get any paramedic we could, and now to finally have a decision that felt right and for us to go with was a huge motivator, not only for me to try harder to catch up, but the whole team.

One of the key considerations when choosing concept 3 was through our overall heuristic results. This clearly showed to us that in terms of usability (based on design concepts and principles), our Dr Drone app concept was the strongest. Adding on to this, the overall clear strength of our proposed Dr. Drone App (accumulated from synthesis of insights from user testing) can be outlined below:

  • The perspective of the third concept is on the patients point of view, which leveraged more ways and showed more potentials in which we could lighten the load and stress that paramedics are already facing.
  • The concept was unique in that it aimed to make individuals more proactive about their health and to potentially double check their need for an ambulance before commiting to the service. This is able to attempt to lighten the demands of 000 calls, and hence overall contributed to strengthening this concept’s potentials
  • This idea was also chosen based on overall positive feedback from users and our ability to test it more accurately than the other two concepts.
  • This is explored further with the decision matrix in the next slide, showing that the Dr. Drone concept scored the highest score.

On top of all the above considerations that made concept 3 a winning one in our eyes, my team and I felt more excited about taking this concept and further developing it to the final stage. As a result, we felt more of a connection to our third concept, and the fact that it also perfectly addressed our problem statement was a winning point for our team. We were now recovering from our sheer panic stage and were feeling amped up and ready to rapidly prototype a medium fidelity version of the Dr. Drone app for a final round of user testing for this stage of the design process.

However I noticed that our concept 3 ticked all the points, which was a fun moment to share with the team and a nice indication that we were finally on track and in a productive state, which I am grateful for. This checklist included:

  • Improving traffic and road conditions

8. The final leg of the journey!

The biggest and most stressful chunk of our design process so far was now completed and we as a team could now look forward to a clearer end goal. Admittedly, or at least in my perspective, our team was starting to get a bit lost in our problem space upon developing our concepts for user testing. Now, at this point, looking back, I can see that we may have ended up straying away from our problem statement if we were to continue our first two original concepts that required the paramedics to be our primary user testers. Particularly as we did indeed come across several big and small issues from user testing concepts 1 and 2. So i’m even more grateful and glad that our team faced the challenges that we did, and adapted to it as best as we could. In doing so, I was able to gain a very eye-opening valuable insight into the huge potential space in our problem context of directly empowering patients/bystanders. I personally love it when I take a life problem and realise the other side of the story, which I tend to believe always has a real empowering effect for effective change. I also realised that by placing a sense of control on patients/bystanders, this act in itself would decrease the overall huge burden that paramedics will always have in this critical industry.

Our team then buckled down and worked out the general theme of the high fidelity mockup. Once we talked it over with each other, and an android-look approach was taken, our team took this information (as well as the feedback from our medium fidelity) and began to build up the first high fidelity look. NOTE: This was actually extremely enjoyable to make (along with the next two versions), as we finally had a very confident vision of what we were trying to make and how it should feel for the consumer. It was a relief in comparison to the last assessment.

The Android Version or Version 1 of the high fidelity was met really well. Upon testing the interface look on people, there were some general concerns over the simplicity of the theme. Also, it did have some set back as most of our users were iOS users. It also didn’t feel like a high fidelity app (due to colour scheme, but also the lack of pages to help the flow). This feedback was reinforced by Ashlyn as well, but we still wanted to test it after that to ensure we were at least on the right track of functionality. This is when, after consultation, we pivoted to an iOS layout and design structure! The feedback we got from our first iteration proved to be very critical in the pivot and development of version 2.

Figure 8.1 - Using affinity diagrams to synthesise our results for all 3 concepts

Version 2, the new iOS version. Personally I loved this version, and overall thought my team were doing well. We were extremely happy with the new look. It provided better flow and overall improved aesthetics that pushed it into the high fidelity category (unlike version 1). The new version was met extremely well during our co-discovery testing. The users had many compliments, but also did discover some bugs in the user flow (lack of buttons and some screens). 3 of the users had issues with the amount of text for the situation at hand; “who would read that much text when they are hurt?” and “make the emergency call confirmation a simple “are you sure?” rather than being long winded”. We had the addition of a new button that looked like an asterisk which was meant to act as a back button that saves the previous page you were on. Most people told us to cut it or they didn’t understand so it quietly disappeared never to be seen again for version 3.

Our final and best version, number 3 is now created. We poured everything we have learned up until this point to do the final, refining and aesthetic alterations before the presenting of our solution. We at this point are extremely proud and happy to be finished. Each of us really pulled our weight to make all this happen. It was a nice ending from a set of two assessments that went from extremely stressful to extremely satisfying as we patted each other on back and cheered complete.

Overall from this process, I believe I have become a much better designer. As this is actually my first studio in this degree, I felt a lot of the stress and overall found it a much bigger challenge than any class I had had before (but it was very rewarding!). In this assessment, particularly by ending with the creation of the high fidelity, I finally felt comfortable with everything I had learnt in design so far. I feel I have much more to learn, but the sense of accomplishment is great. I’d like to thank Ashlyn for being a huge support to my team and I. So thanks to her for uplifting us when we needed it, and sharing her wisdom to us, as well as sharing our moment of stress (thanks for pitying us during this time haha!). That is the conclusion to my process journal and my perspective of the creation of Dr. Drone.
